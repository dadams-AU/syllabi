% ========== SECTION 5: COURSE DESCRIPTION ==========
\section*{Course Description}

{\setlength{\parindent}{0pt}\setlength{\parskip}{0.6\baselineskip}%
This course is an intensive introduction to U.S. social welfare policy and politics. It has four parts: the big picture (who are the poor, and how did the welfare state develop?); the programs (social insurance, the safety net, tax expenditures); two case studies in major reform (welfare reform and the ACA); and the political forces that shape all of it (public opinion, interest groups, race, and gender).

The course moves between levels of analysis. Comparative welfare state theory sits alongside individual family narratives; legislative history sits alongside street-level program design. Both matter. You cannot understand why TANF looks the way it does without understanding the politics that produced it, and you cannot evaluate whether the ACA succeeded without understanding what it was actually trying to accomplish.

This course is taught from a political science and policy analysis perspective. Political science helps explain \emph{why} we get the policies we get; policy analysis helps evaluate \emph{whether those policies do what they claim}. Both lenses are at work throughout the semester.
}

% ========== SECTION 6: STUDENT LEARNING OUTCOMES ==========
\section*{Student Learning Outcomes}

{\setlength{\parindent}{0pt}\setlength{\parskip}{0.6\baselineskip}%
By the end of the semester, students will be able to:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Describe the core architecture of the U.S. welfare state: social insurance, public assistance, tax expenditures, and public-private arrangements.
    \item Trace the political development of major programs (Social Security, TANF, Medicaid, ACA) and connect that history to current policy debates.
    \item Apply key concepts from political science and policy analysis to evaluate welfare programs: policy feedback, institutional design, interest group politics, and public opinion.
    \item Analyze how race and gender have structured both program design and access to benefits.
    \item Situate U.S. welfare policy within a comparative frame and explain the sources of American exceptionalism.
    \item Construct an evidence-based policy argument using primary sources and scholarly literature.
\end{enumerate}
}

% ========== SECTION 9: COURSE REQUIREMENTS ==========
\section*{Course Requirements}

\subsection*{Course Format}
This is an in-person lecture and discussion course meeting twice weekly for 75 minutes. Students complete assigned readings before class; class time is divided between lecture, structured discussion, and periodic documentary screenings. Three weeks are structured as asynchronous work weeks with no in-person meetings (see below).

\subsection*{Three Asynchronous Weeks}
Three weeks are designated asynchronous. There are no in-person meetings those weeks. Students complete assigned readings, watch a designated documentary, and submit a Canvas response by that Friday at 11:59 p.m. Specific instructions will be posted to Canvas in advance.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Week 4 (Async):} Political development of the welfare state; \emph{Growing Up Poor in America}, Part 2
    \item \textbf{Week 9 (Async):} Welfare reform; \emph{Two American Families}
    \item \textbf{Week 14 (Async):} Public opinion and interest groups; term papers and grad outlines due
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Graded Work}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Required for all students (60\% total):} Attendance and Participation (10\%); Discussion Papers, 5 of 10 (10\%); Midterm Exam (20\%); Final Exam (20\%).
    \item \textbf{Undergraduates only (40\% total):}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Policy Brief (20\%):} A 3--4 page analysis of one program: OASDI, SSI, TANF, Unemployment Insurance, Medicaid, Medicare, SNAP, or the EITC. Cover the program's stated purpose, who it serves, its major design features, and one significant reform debate. Academic writing conventions and source citations required.
        \item \textbf{Term Paper (20\%):} A 6--8 page research paper building on the policy brief. Analyze the political and policy-relevant strengths and weaknesses of your program and develop a reform proposal, drawing on course concepts and the program's political history.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Graduate students only (40\% total):}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Research Proposal (10\%):} Topic, research question, and preliminary bibliography.
        \item \textbf{Introduction/Outline/Annotated Bibliography (10\%):} Substantial revision from the proposal with clear research design and sources.
        \item \textbf{Final Research Paper (20\%):} A 10--15 page original research paper submitted during finals week.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Graduate Student Requirements}
Graduate students complete all readings including graduate-only readings posted on Canvas. They take both the midterm and final exams and write five discussion papers. In place of the undergraduate policy brief and term paper, graduate students complete a scaffolded 10--15 page original research paper sequence with staged feedback and revision across three milestones:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Research Proposal (10\%)} (1--2 pages): Due Week 7, October 5.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Identify policy topic, core research question, and why the question matters for welfare politics/policy.
        \item Include a working thesis or argument direction.
        \item Include a preliminary bibliography (minimum 6 credible sources, including scholarly sources).
        \item Instructor feedback will focus on scope, feasibility, and argument focus.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Introduction, Outline, and Annotated Bibliography (10\%):} Due Week 14, December 2.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Revised introduction with clear thesis and paper roadmap.
        \item Detailed section outline showing claim flow and supporting evidence for each section.
        \item Annotated bibliography with brief annotations describing each source's argument and relevance to your project.
        \item Substantial revision from the proposal is expected; this milestone should show a sharpened question and stronger evidence base.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Final Research Paper (20\%)} (10--15 pages): Due during finals week.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Original analysis that synthesizes course concepts, scholarly literature, and policy evidence.
        \item Clear argument structure (thesis, evidence, analysis, counterargument/limitations, conclusion).
        \item Proper citation and academic writing conventions.
        \item The final submission should directly incorporate and respond to feedback from Milestones 1 and 2.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

% ========== SECTION 12: POLICY ON GENERATIVE AI ==========
\section*{Policy on the Use of Generative AI and Other Technology}

Generative AI (including large language models, image generators, and other tools) is permitted in this course, but use must be transparent, intentional, and in service of learning. The core principle is simple: \textbf{you must do the intellectual work of this course}. AI can amplify your thinking, but not replace it.

\subsection*{Course-supported AI environment: PapyrusAI}

This course uses \textbf{PapyrusAI} (\href{https://www.genaied.org/papyrusai.html}{genaied.org/papyrusai}) as the supported AI environment for graded writing. PapyrusAI is an instructor-configured, contained LLM environment: I build the modules, set the source materials the AI may draw on, and define how it interacts with you. Modules in this course are designed to \emph{coach}, not to write for you---they will ask questions, push back on weak claims, and point you to readings, but they will not produce paragraphs you can paste into an assignment.

Specific PapyrusAI modules will accompany the policy brief, term paper, graduate research milestones, discussion papers, and the asynchronous-week documentary responses. \textbf{Access PapyrusAI from the left-hand navigation menu inside our Canvas course site}---no separate login or account setup is required. Use of these modules is encouraged but not required; they exist to make productive AI use easy to access and unproductive use harder to fall into.

You may also use general-purpose AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.) outside PapyrusAI subject to the rules below. The permitted/not-permitted list applies equally regardless of which tool you use.

\subsection*{Permitted uses:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Brainstorming and outlining arguments
\item Explaining concepts you don't understand (then explaining it back in your own words)
\item Literature searching and summarizing sources
\item Editing, proofreading, and revising your work
\item Sanity-checking your analysis or logic
\item Generating synthetic examples or test cases for your ideas
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Not permitted:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Using AI to generate your analysis, arguments, or conclusions
\item Submitting AI-generated text as your own writing
\item Using AI to avoid engaging with course concepts or readings
\item Letting AI do the intellectual heavy lifting (interpreting sources, building arguments, synthesizing ideas)
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Baseline writing diagnostic:}
During Week 2, you will complete a short in-class writing exercise (unaided, handwritten or in a locked-down Canvas window) responding to a course prompt. This is not graded for content and counts toward participation. Its purpose is to establish a baseline sample of your own writing voice early in the semester, before AI-supported assignments begin.

\subsection*{Disclosure requirement:}
If you use AI tools in ways beyond basic editing, you must disclose your use. For work completed inside a PapyrusAI course module, the module generates a transparency log you can attach to your submission---this satisfies disclosure automatically. For use outside PapyrusAI, include a brief note at the end of your assignment explaining what tools you used and how (e.g., ``I used Claude to help organize my outline and check the logic of my argument in Section 3''). This is not a confession---it's transparency about your process.

\subsection*{What this means:}
The goal of this course is for \emph{you} to learn to think like a policy analyst and to develop your own informed arguments about welfare policy. AI is a tool that can enhance that learning if used thoughtfully. Using it to avoid thinking will undermine your own education and violates academic integrity. Questions about what constitutes appropriate use? Ask before you submit.
