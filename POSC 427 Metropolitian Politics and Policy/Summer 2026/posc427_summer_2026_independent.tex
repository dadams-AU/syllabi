% ========== SECTION 5: OVERVIEW ==========
\section*{Overview}

{\setlength{\parindent}{0pt}\setlength{\parskip}{0.6\baselineskip}%
This five-week independent study introduces major theories and institutional frameworks associated with metropolitan governance and policymaking. The course focuses on fragmentation, polycentric governance, intergovernmental relations, and regional policy coordination.

The independent study also serves as a pilot version of a future full-semester course on metropolitan governance (POSC 427). The central question guiding the work is:

\begin{quote}
\textit{How do fragmented metropolitan systems govern shared regional problems under conditions of inequality, interdependence, and institutional complexity?}
\end{quote}
}

% ========== SECTION 6: STUDENT LEARNING OUTCOMES ==========
\section*{Student Learning Outcomes}

{\setlength{\parindent}{0pt}\setlength{\parskip}{0.6\baselineskip}%
By the end of the independent study, the student should be able to:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Explain major approaches to metropolitan governance.
    \item Analyze the institutional consequences of fragmentation.
    \item Apply polycentric governance frameworks to metropolitan policy problems.
    \item Evaluate regional governance challenges in Southern California.
\end{enumerate}
}

% ========== SECTION 11: POLICY ON GENERATIVE AI ==========
\section*{Policy on the Use of Generative AI and Other Technology}

Generative AI (including large language models, image generators, and other tools) is permitted in this course, but use must be transparent, intentional, and in service of learning. The core principle is simple: \textbf{you must do the intellectual work of this course}. AI can amplify your thinking, but not replace it.

\subsection*{Course-supported AI environment: PapyrusAI}

This course uses \textbf{PapyrusAI} (\href{https://www.genaied.org/papyrusai.html}{genaied.org/papyrusai}) as the supported AI environment for graded writing. PapyrusAI is an instructor-configured, contained LLM environment: I build the modules, set the source materials the AI may draw on, and define how it interacts with you. Modules in this course are designed to \emph{coach}, not to write for you---they will ask questions, push back on weak claims, and point you to readings, but they will not produce paragraphs you can paste into an assignment.

Specific PapyrusAI modules accompany the weekly reflections, the governance mapping exercise, and the final analysis. \textbf{Access PapyrusAI from the left-hand navigation menu inside our Canvas course site}---no separate login or account setup is required. Use of these modules is encouraged but not required.

You may also use general-purpose AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.) outside PapyrusAI subject to the rules below. The permitted/not-permitted list applies equally regardless of which tool you use.

\subsection*{Permitted uses:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Brainstorming and outlining arguments
\item Explaining concepts you don't understand (then explaining it back in your own words)
\item Literature searching and summarizing sources
\item Editing, proofreading, and revising your work
\item Sanity-checking your analysis or logic
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Not permitted:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Using AI to generate your analysis, arguments, or conclusions
\item Submitting AI-generated text as your own writing
\item Using AI to avoid engaging with course concepts or readings
\item Letting AI do the intellectual heavy lifting (interpreting sources, building arguments, synthesizing ideas)
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Disclosure requirement:}
If you use AI tools in ways beyond basic editing, you must disclose your use. For work completed inside a PapyrusAI module, the module generates a transparency log you can attach to your submission. For use outside PapyrusAI, include a brief note at the end of your assignment explaining what tools you used and how. This is transparency about your process, not a confession.
