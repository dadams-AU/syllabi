% ========== SECTION 5: COURSE DESCRIPTION ==========
\section*{Course Description}

{\setlength{\parindent}{0pt}\setlength{\parskip}{0.6\baselineskip}%
Metropolitan politics is the study of how fragmented systems attempt to govern interdependent regional problems. This course examines the governance, politics, and policymaking dynamics of metropolitan regions in the United States, with particular emphasis on fragmentation, intergovernmental relations, regional coordination, institutional complexity, and collective action across urban and suburban jurisdictions.

While the course engages classic urban politics scholarship, it places particular emphasis on metropolitan governance, institutional fragmentation, federalism, collaborative governance, and the institutional challenges associated with governing complex regional systems.

Students will examine how metropolitan regions attempt to govern shared policy problems including housing, transportation, environmental governance, public safety, economic development, homelessness, and climate adaptation. The course uses Southern California as a recurring empirical case while situating metropolitan governance within broader debates in political science and public administration.

The central question guiding the course is:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Why is metropolitan governance in Southern California so fragmented, and how does anything get solved anyway?}
\end{quote}
}

% ========== SECTION 9: COURSE REQUIREMENTS ==========
\section*{Course Requirements}

\subsection*{Course Format}
This is an in-person lecture and discussion course. Students complete assigned readings before class; class time is divided between lecture and structured discussion of metropolitan governance theory and Southern California cases.

\subsection*{Graded Work}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Undergraduates:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Metropolitan Governance Profile (20\%):} A structured profile of one metropolitan region---its jurisdictions, governing institutions, and the principal regional bodies that coordinate (or fail to coordinate) shared problems.
        \item \textbf{Institutional Mapping Memo (20\%):} A memo mapping the institutions, actors, and intergovernmental relationships that bear on one regional policy problem, with analysis of where fragmentation helps and hinders.
        \item \textbf{Midterm Examination (20\%):} In-class examination covering Part I and the early Part II material.
        \item \textbf{Final Metropolitan Policy Memo (25\%):} A policy memo developing evidence-based recommendations for a regional governance problem, building on the profile and mapping memo.
        \item \textbf{Participation and Discussion (15\%):} Active, prepared engagement in class discussion.
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Graduate students:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Institutional Mapping Memo (15\%):} As above.
        \item \textbf{Graduate Analytical Memos (20\%):} Four short analytical memos engaging the graduate readings and metropolitan governance theory.
        \item \textbf{Discussion Leadership (10\%):} Lead one class session's discussion of the assigned and graduate readings.
        \item \textbf{Midterm Examination (20\%):} As above.
        \item \textbf{Final Institutional Analysis Paper (25\%):} A 12--15 page original institutional analysis paper, in place of the undergraduate policy memo.
        \item \textbf{Participation and Discussion (10\%):} As above.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Graduate Student Requirements}
Graduate students complete additional theoretical readings (the Graduate Reading selections in the schedule and graduate-only readings posted on Canvas) and written analytical assignments designed to deepen engagement with metropolitan governance theory, institutional analysis, and collaborative governance. In place of the undergraduate Metropolitan Governance Profile and Final Metropolitan Policy Memo, graduate students complete four analytical memos, one discussion leadership assignment, and a scaffolded institutional analysis paper:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Graduate Analytical Memos (20\%):} Four short memos across the semester, each engaging a graduate reading and connecting theory to a metropolitan governance problem.
    \item \textbf{Discussion Leadership (10\%):} Lead one session, framing the readings and guiding discussion.
    \item \textbf{Final Institutional Analysis Paper (25\%)} (12--15 pages): Due during finals week. Original analysis synthesizing course theory, scholarly literature, and evidence on a metropolitan governance problem, with a clear argument structure and proper citation.
\end{enumerate}

% ========== SECTION 12: POLICY ON GENERATIVE AI ==========
\section*{Policy on the Use of Generative AI and Other Technology}

Generative AI (including large language models, image generators, and other tools) is permitted in this course, but use must be transparent, intentional, and in service of learning. The core principle is simple: \textbf{you must do the intellectual work of this course}. AI can amplify your thinking, but not replace it.

\subsection*{Course-supported AI environment: PapyrusAI}

This course uses \textbf{PapyrusAI} (\href{https://www.genaied.org/papyrusai.html}{genaied.org/papyrusai}) as the supported AI environment for graded writing. PapyrusAI is an instructor-configured, contained LLM environment: I build the modules, set the source materials the AI may draw on, and define how it interacts with you. Modules in this course are designed to \emph{coach}, not to write for you---they will ask questions, push back on weak claims, and point you to readings, but they will not produce paragraphs you can paste into an assignment.

Specific PapyrusAI modules accompany the metropolitan governance profile, institutional mapping memo, final policy memo, and graduate analytical memos and research paper. \textbf{Access PapyrusAI from the left-hand navigation menu inside our Canvas course site}---no separate login or account setup is required. Use of these modules is encouraged but not required; they exist to make productive AI use easy to access and unproductive use harder to fall into.

You may also use general-purpose AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.) outside PapyrusAI subject to the rules below. The permitted/not-permitted list applies equally regardless of which tool you use.

\subsection*{Permitted uses:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Brainstorming and outlining arguments
\item Explaining concepts you don't understand (then explaining it back in your own words)
\item Literature searching and summarizing sources
\item Editing, proofreading, and revising your work
\item Sanity-checking your analysis or logic
\item Generating synthetic examples or test cases for your ideas
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Not permitted:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Using AI to generate your analysis, arguments, or conclusions
\item Submitting AI-generated text as your own writing
\item Using AI to avoid engaging with course concepts or readings
\item Letting AI do the intellectual heavy lifting (interpreting sources, building arguments, synthesizing ideas)
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Disclosure requirement:}
If you use AI tools in ways beyond basic editing, you must disclose your use. For work completed inside a PapyrusAI course module, the module generates a transparency log you can attach to your submission---this satisfies disclosure automatically. For use outside PapyrusAI, include a brief note at the end of your assignment explaining what tools you used and how (e.g., ``I used Claude to help organize my outline and check the logic of my argument in Section 3''). This is not a confession---it's transparency about your process.

\subsection*{What this means:}
The goal of this course is for \emph{you} to learn to think like a policy analyst and to develop your own informed arguments about metropolitan governance. AI is a tool that can enhance that learning if used thoughtfully. Using it to avoid thinking will undermine your own education and violates academic integrity. Questions about what constitutes appropriate use? Ask before you submit.
