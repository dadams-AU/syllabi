AI-Supported Scholarship Workflow

Annotated Bibliography, Synthesis, Literature Review, and Reflection

This page outlines a practical way to integrate AI into reading- and writing-intensive courses without outsourcing student thinking. In this model, students do the reading, drafting, analysis, and revision themselves. AI is used only to provide critique, surface missing connections, and support reflection on the learning process.

Core principle: Students may use AI to ask for feedback, questions, and critique. Students may not use AI to draft, rewrite, summarize, or polish work they submit as their own.

Why Use This Assignment Structure?

  • It helps students move from summary to synthesis.
  • It encourages closer reading and stronger source evaluation.
  • It gives students a structured way to use AI for critique rather than substitution.
  • It builds metacognitive habits through reflection and next-step planning.
  • It scales from weekly reading responses to a larger literature review.

Recommended Workflow

1. Annotated Bibliography

For each reading or source, students prepare a short annotation that includes:

  • A full citation in the required style.
  • A concise summary of the central argument and key points.
  • A note on relevance to the course topic, research question, or public/professional problem.

For many courses, a 125- to 150-word annotation per source works well.

Appropriate AI Use

  • Check whether the summary appears accurate.
  • Identify missing concepts or overlooked terms.
  • Test whether the relevance statement is clear and specific.
  • Generate one discussion question based on the source.

2. Synthesis Paper or Literature Review

Students then use their annotations to write either a short weekly synthesis or a larger literature review. The goal is not to stack summaries, but to analyze relationships across sources.

  • Identify patterns, themes, and recurring debates.
  • Draw connections across authors, cases, or frameworks.
  • Address contradictions, tensions, or gaps in the literature.
  • Connect theory to practice, policy, or professional application.
  • For a literature review, organize the paper around a clear research question or problem.

Appropriate AI Use

  • Request critique of reasoning, evidence, and organization.
  • Ask for possible counterarguments or blind spots.
  • Ask what cross-source connections may be underdeveloped.
  • Use feedback to plan future improvement.

3. Reflection and Next-Step Planning

After drafting and receiving feedback, students complete a short reflection. This step turns AI use into a documented learning process rather than an invisible shortcut.

  • What changed in your understanding after writing?
  • Which feedback was most useful, and why?
  • What was still unclear or difficult?
  • What will you do differently in the next assignment or revision cycle?

Suggested Submission Components

  1. The student-written annotated bibliography, synthesis, or literature review.
  2. A short feedback appendix that includes the AI prompt used and a brief excerpt or summary of the response.
  3. A short reflection or next-step plan describing how the student evaluated the feedback.

AI Use Boundaries

Allowed Not Allowed
Ask AI to critique a summary for accuracy or missing concepts. Ask AI to write the annotation.
Ask AI to identify gaps, counterarguments, or underdeveloped connections. Ask AI to draft, rewrite, or polish paragraphs for submission.
Use AI to generate discussion questions or reflection questions. Paste AI-generated prose into student work without attribution and permission.
Use AI feedback to set goals for the next assignment. Use AI to replace the student's reading, analysis, or judgment.

Sample Prompts

Prompt for an Annotated Bibliography Entry

You are my writing coach for a reading-intensive course. Please review one annotated bibliography entry. Check for accuracy of the summary, clarity of the relevance statement, and any missing concepts from the reading. Return: (1) 3 bullet critiques, (2) 1 suggestion for improvement, and (3) 1 discussion question. Do not rewrite my text or propose replacement sentences.

Prompt for a Synthesis Paper or Literature Review

Act as a critical reviewer for my draft. Identify 3 strengths, 3 gaps in reasoning or evidence, 2 counterarguments I should address, and 2 additional connections across sources that I may have missed. Do not rewrite my paper or propose replacement text.

Prompt for Reflection

I have written a short reflection on my learning process during this assignment. Please respond with: (1) 2 questions that push my thinking further, (2) 2 observations about what I seem to be learning, and (3) 1 suggestion for what I should try next time. Do not rewrite my reflection.

Implementation Notes for Instructors

  • Keep the boundary simple: feedback and critique are allowed; drafting and rewriting are not.
  • Require students to submit the prompt they used if AI feedback is part of the assignment.
  • Consider making AI feedback optional for early low-stakes work and required for later reflective work.
  • Use short appendices rather than long transcripts.
  • Assess the student's writing and thinking, not the quality of the AI output.
  • For larger literature reviews, ask students to use AI to identify gaps and debates, then explain which suggestions they accepted or rejected.

Adaptation Ideas

  • In weekly reading courses, use the full annotated bibliography → synthesis → reflection sequence.
  • In research methods or capstone courses, extend the model into a longer literature review workflow.
  • In professional programs, ask students to connect the literature to policy, administration, organizational practice, or equity concerns.

Bottom line: This assignment design preserves student authorship while giving students a concrete, transparent, and academically responsible way to use AI as a feedback tool.
